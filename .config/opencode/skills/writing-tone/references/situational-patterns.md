# Situational Patterns

Niche post types pulled out of the always-loaded core. Load when drafting one of these.

## Voluntary-support offerings (office hours, drop-in time, ad-hoc pairing)

- Frame each benefit from the recipient's view of your limitation, not your scarcity ('less awkwardness when I miss your review requests' not 'less rationing of my brain across reviews and threads'). Avoid benefits that read as managerial assessment (leveling reads, performance calibration); those belong in 1:1s and undercut the low-pressure frame.
- List concrete artifact/format types to bring ('code', 'a design doc', 'PR review', 'a decision that needs a second opinion'), not adjective-qualified emotional labels ('a thorny review', 'a tough call'). The reader needs to recognise the format, not be persuaded their thing qualifies.
- Soften prescriptive negation with 'not intended as' ('this is not intended as a status check or 1:1, so no prep expected') over parallel negation ('not a status check, not a 1:1'), which reads as a rule about what they can't do.
- Bound the time by your own constraint, not only the task ('60 to 90 minutes, or however long the task needs or my time allows'). Acknowledging your own bandwidth is more sustainable than open-ended promises.
- Leave the default tool/format open ('pop for screen-share (or what you prefer)') rather than locking it with a cited justification ('retro said you prefer it over Tuple'), which makes it feel non-negotiable.
- Own the follow-up-review schedule yourself ('I will revisit in a month to see if I'm ready to increase the commitment') rather than outsourcing the trigger ('tell me when you think it's time to step up'), which leaves the cadence frozen.
- Use a complete subject-verb closer ('I'll be booking the first slots this week' not 'Booking the first slots this week').

## Product-share / community-announce (forum launch, tool reveal, plugin beta)

Order: Disclaimer (if any) → Why I made it → What it does → What would help most from testers / how to engage → Install / get started → Links → AI disclaimer (if any). Personal motivation leads; the AI disclaimer trails as a footer, never a headline banner. See worked example 12 in `style-examples.md`.

- Open 'Why' with a personal pain point ('I got sick of looking for iOS workout trackers that weren't buggy or full of ads') before any abstract benefit or design rationale.
- Frame known gaps as audience questions ('there are some known missing features like repeat workouts that I don't use, but would like to know if they'd be useful to others'), not apologies ('sorry I haven't built X') or roadmap promises ('X is on the roadmap for v2').
- AI disclosure stays minimal: tools used, ownership claim ('I read every diff', 'I set the architecture'), response commitment ('you're talking to me, not a bot'). Cut safety-net detail (adversarial passes, CI gates, lint/format); that belongs in a CONTRIBUTING file.
- Image captions: friendly and functional, naming what's shown plus one beat of personality ('Workout editor, designed to be mobile friendly too' not 'Workout editor screenshot'); single line, no trailing period, no marketing verbs ('beautifully crafted', 'powerful').
